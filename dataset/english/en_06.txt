Agriculture and Food Security
Agriculture
Agriculture is the oldest and most fundamental human activity, providing the food that sustains all other human endeavors. Modern farming techniques have dramatically increased crop yields, allowing a growing global population to be fed from a relatively stable land area. However, agriculture also places enormous pressure on natural resources, consuming the majority of the world's freshwater supply and contributing significantly to greenhouse gas emissions.

Farmers work the land to produce the grains, vegetables, fruits, and animal products that form the basis of human diets worldwide. Smallholder farmers, who work small plots of land, produce a significant share of the food consumed in developing countries. Irrigation systems allow farming in dry regions, while improved crop varieties resist disease and produce higher yields. Fertilizers and pesticides boost productivity but can also damage soil health and water quality when overused.

Food security — ensuring that all people have reliable access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food — is a central concern of agricultural policy worldwide. Hunger and malnutrition remain serious problems in many parts of the world, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. Climate change threatens to disrupt agricultural production by altering rainfall patterns, increasing extreme weather events, and shifting growing seasons.

Sustainable agriculture seeks to maintain food production while protecting natural resources and reducing environmental impacts. Practices such as crop rotation, agroforestry, conservation tillage, and integrated pest management help maintain soil health, reduce water use, and preserve biodiversity. Connecting farmers to markets, credit, and extension services is essential for improving agricultural productivity and livelihoods.
