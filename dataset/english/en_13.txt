Food, Nutrition, and Healthy Eating
Food
Food is far more than mere fuel for the body — it is a source of pleasure, a marker of identity, a medium for social connection, and a product of complex agricultural, industrial, and cultural systems. What we eat profoundly affects our health, our environment, and the livelihoods of farmers, food workers, and communities around the world. Understanding nutrition — the science of how food affects the body — is essential for making choices that support long-term health and well-being.

A healthy diet provides the energy, protein, fats, carbohydrates, vitamins, and minerals that the body needs to function well. Fruits and vegetables are rich in vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants that reduce the risk of chronic disease. Whole grains provide sustained energy and dietary fiber that supports digestive health. Lean proteins — from legumes, fish, poultry, and dairy — supply the amino acids needed for growth and repair. Excessive consumption of processed foods, sugary drinks, salt, and saturated fats is linked to obesity, heart disease, diabetes, and other chronic conditions.

Malnutrition takes multiple forms: undernutrition (insufficient calories and nutrients), micronutrient deficiency (lack of specific vitamins and minerals), and overnutrition (excessive calorie consumption). All three forms are widespread and cause significant health burdens. Food insecurity — lack of reliable access to sufficient nutritious food — affects hundreds of millions of people worldwide and is linked to poverty, conflict, and climate change.

Food systems — from farm to fork — have enormous environmental footprints, consuming water and energy, generating greenhouse gases, and affecting land use and biodiversity. Shifting toward diets that are healthier for people and more sustainable for the planet is one of the most important challenges facing humanity in the coming decades.
